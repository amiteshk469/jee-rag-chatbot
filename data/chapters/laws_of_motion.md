# Laws of Motion — JEE Physics

## 1. Introduction

Dynamics is the branch of mechanics that deals with the study of motion along with the forces causing it. While kinematics describes "how" an object moves, dynamics explains "why" it moves. Newton's Laws of Motion form the foundation of classical mechanics.

### Force
A force is a push or pull that can change the state of rest or motion of a body. It is a **vector quantity**.

**Effects of Force:**
- Can change the speed of an object
- Can change the direction of motion
- Can change the shape of an object
- SI unit: Newton (N)
- 1 N = 1 kg⋅m/s²

### Types of Forces
- **Contact Forces**: Normal force, friction, tension, spring force
- **Non-contact Forces**: Gravitational, electromagnetic, nuclear

---

## 2. Newton's First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)

**Statement:** Every body continues in its state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line unless compelled by an external unbalanced force to change that state.

### Inertia
Inertia is the property of a body by which it resists any change in its state of rest or uniform motion. Mass is a measure of inertia — greater the mass, greater the inertia.

### Types of Inertia
1. **Inertia of Rest**: A body at rest tends to remain at rest
   - Example: Passengers jerk forward when a bus suddenly stops
   - Example: Dust comes off a carpet when beaten

2. **Inertia of Motion**: A moving body tends to continue moving with same velocity
   - Example: A person jumping from a moving bus falls forward
   - Example: An athlete runs before taking a long jump

3. **Inertia of Direction**: A body tends to maintain its direction of motion
   - Example: Sparks fly tangentially from a grinding wheel
   - Example: Mud flies off a rotating tire tangentially

### Significance of First Law
- Defines the concept of inertia
- Defines force qualitatively
- Defines inertial frames of reference (frames where Newton's laws are valid)

---

## 3. Newton's Second Law of Motion

**Statement:** The rate of change of linear momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied external force and takes place in the direction of the force.

**Mathematical Form:**
F = dp/dt

Where p = mv (linear momentum)

For constant mass:
**F = ma**

Where:
- F = net external force (N)
- m = mass of object (kg)
- a = acceleration produced (m/s²)

### Key Points
1. F = ma is the operational definition of force
2. Acceleration is always in the direction of the net force
3. If F = 0, then a = 0 (body is in equilibrium — either at rest or moving with constant velocity)
4. This is a vector equation: F_x = ma_x, F_y = ma_y, F_z = ma_z
5. Force is the cause, acceleration is the effect

### Impulse
When a large force acts for a very short time, the product of force and time is called impulse.

**Impulse (J) = F × Δt = Δp = change in momentum**

- Impulse is a vector quantity
- SI unit: N⋅s or kg⋅m/s
- Example: A batsman hitting a cricket ball applies large force for short time
- Area under F-t graph = impulse = change in momentum

### Impulse-Momentum Theorem
The impulse of a force on a body equals the change in momentum of the body.
J = p₂ - p₁ = mv₂ - mv₁

## 4. Newton's Third Law of Motion

**Statement:** To every action, there is always an equal and opposite reaction. Action and reaction forces act on different bodies.

**Mathematical Form:** F₁₂ = -F₂₁

### Key Points
1. Action and reaction are simultaneous
2. They act on different bodies (never cancel each other)
3. They are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction
4. They always occur in pairs — you cannot have one without the other
5. Newton's third law is valid in all frames (inertial and non-inertial)

### Examples
- Walking: You push the ground backward, ground pushes you forward
- Rocket propulsion: Exhaust gases push backward, rocket moves forward
- Swimming: Swimmer pushes water backward, water pushes swimmer forward
- Book on table: Book pushes table down (weight), table pushes book up (normal reaction)

### Important: Action-Reaction vs. Balanced Forces
- Action-reaction act on DIFFERENT bodies
- Balanced forces act on the SAME body
- A book on a table: Weight and normal force are NOT action-reaction pairs (they act on the same body). The action-reaction pair of the book's weight is the gravitational pull of the book on the Earth.

## 5. Linear Momentum and Conservation

### Linear Momentum
**p = mv**

- Vector quantity
- Direction: same as velocity
- SI unit: kg⋅m/s

### Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum
If no external force acts on a system, the total momentum of the system remains constant.

**If F_ext = 0, then p = constant**

For a system of two objects:
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂

### Applications
1. **Recoil of Gun**: m_bullet × v_bullet = -m_gun × v_gun
   - Gun recoils with velocity = -(m_bullet/m_gun) × v_bullet
   
2. **Rocket Propulsion**: Thrust = v_exhaust × (dm/dt)
   - As fuel mass decreases, rocket accelerates

3. **Collision Problems**: Total momentum before = Total momentum after

## 6. Equilibrium of a Particle

A particle is in equilibrium when the net force acting on it is zero.

**Condition:** ΣF = 0

This means: ΣFₓ = 0, ΣFᵧ = 0, ΣFᵤ = 0

### Concurrent Forces
Forces whose lines of action pass through a single point are called concurrent forces.

### Lami's Theorem
If three concurrent coplanar forces keep a body in equilibrium, then each force is proportional to the sine of the angle between the other two forces.

F₁/sin α = F₂/sin β = F₃/sin γ

Where α, β, γ are the angles opposite to forces F₁, F₂, F₃ respectively.

## 7. Common Forces in Mechanics

### 7.1 Weight (Gravitational Force)
W = mg (directed vertically downward toward center of Earth)

### 7.2 Normal Force (N)
- Perpendicular to the contact surface
- Prevents objects from passing through surfaces
- Adjusts itself to maintain contact
- On a horizontal surface: N = mg (for no other vertical forces)
- On an inclined plane: N = mg cosθ

### 7.3 Tension (T)
- Force transmitted through a string, rope, or wire when pulled at both ends
- Acts along the length of the string
- For a massless, inextensible string: tension is same throughout
- For a string with mass: tension varies along its length

### 7.4 Spring Force (Hooke's Law)
**F = -kx**

Where:
- k = spring constant (N/m)
- x = displacement from natural length
- Negative sign indicates restoring nature (force opposes displacement)

## 8. Friction

Friction is a force that opposes the relative motion (or tendency of relative motion) between two surfaces in contact.

### Types of Friction

#### 8.1 Static Friction (fₛ)
- Acts when there is no relative motion between surfaces
- It is a **self-adjusting force** (adjusts to match applied force up to a maximum)
- **Maximum static friction**: fₛ(max) = μₛN
- fₛ ≤ μₛN (it can be anything from 0 to μₛN)
- μₛ = coefficient of static friction

#### 8.2 Kinetic (Sliding) Friction (fₖ)
- Acts when there is relative motion between surfaces
- **fₖ = μₖN** (constant for given surfaces)
- μₖ = coefficient of kinetic friction
- μₖ < μₛ (kinetic friction is less than maximum static friction)

#### 8.3 Rolling Friction
- Occurs when a body rolls over a surface
- Much smaller than sliding friction
- This is why wheels are used in transport

### Properties of Friction
1. Friction is independent of the area of contact (for dry surfaces)
2. Friction depends on the nature of surfaces in contact
3. Static friction ≥ Kinetic friction ≥ Rolling friction
4. μₛ > μₖ for the same pair of surfaces
5. Friction is always parallel to the contact surface

### Angle of Friction (φ)
tan φ = fₛ(max)/N = μₛ

The angle that the resultant of friction and normal force makes with the normal force.

### Angle of Repose (θ)
The maximum angle of an inclined plane at which a body placed on it remains in equilibrium.
tan θ = μₛ
Therefore, angle of repose = angle of friction

## 9. Motion on an Inclined Plane

For a block of mass m on a smooth inclined plane of angle θ:

### Without Friction
- Component of gravity along the plane: mg sinθ (causes motion)
- Component of gravity perpendicular to plane: mg cosθ (balanced by normal force)
- Acceleration down the plane: a = g sinθ
- Normal force: N = mg cosθ

### With Friction
**Case 1: Block sliding down**
- Net force = mg sinθ - fₖ = mg sinθ - μₖ mg cosθ
- Acceleration: a = g(sinθ - μₖ cosθ)
- Block slides if tanθ > μₛ

**Case 2: Block pushed up**
- Net force = F - mg sinθ - fₖ
- Friction acts downward (opposing upward motion)

**Case 3: Block at rest (checking equilibrium)**
- If tanθ ≤ μₛ: block stays at rest, friction = mg sinθ
- If tanθ > μₛ: block starts sliding

## 10. Connected Bodies and Pulley Systems

### Two Bodies Connected by a String (on a surface)
For two blocks m₁ and m₂ connected by a string with applied force F on m₁:
- Acceleration: a = F/(m₁ + m₂)
- Tension: T = m₂F/(m₁ + m₂)

### Atwood Machine (Two masses over a pulley)
For masses m₁ and m₂ (m₁ > m₂) over a massless, frictionless pulley:
- Acceleration: a = (m₁ - m₂)g/(m₁ + m₂)
- Tension: T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁ + m₂)

### Body Hanging from a Scale in an Elevator
**Apparent weight = m(g ± a)**
- Elevator accelerating upward: W_apparent = m(g + a) — feels heavier
- Elevator accelerating downward: W_apparent = m(g - a) — feels lighter
- Elevator in free fall (a = g): W_apparent = 0 — weightlessness
- Elevator at constant velocity: W_apparent = mg — true weight

## 11. Pseudo Forces (Non-Inertial Frames)

In a non-inertial frame (accelerating frame), Newton's laws don't hold directly. We introduce a **pseudo force** (or fictitious force) to apply Newton's laws.

**Pseudo Force = -ma₀** (where a₀ is the acceleration of the frame)

- Acts on every body in the non-inertial frame
- Directed opposite to the acceleration of the frame
- Not a real force (no reaction pair exists)
- Observer in the non-inertial frame perceives it as real

### Examples
- Passengers feel pushed back when a bus accelerates forward
- Objects on a turntable seem to move outward (centrifugal force is a pseudo force)
- Coriolis force on Earth (due to Earth's rotation)

---

## 12. Constraint Equations and Free Body Diagrams

### Free Body Diagram (FBD)
A diagram showing all forces acting on a body, drawn separately for each body in the system.

**Steps to draw FBD:**
1. Identify the body (isolate it from the system)
2. Draw all forces acting ON the body (not BY the body)
3. Include: weight, normal, tension, friction, applied forces
4. Choose a coordinate system
5. Resolve forces into components
6. Apply Newton's second law: ΣF = ma

### Constraint Relations
For connected bodies (strings, pulleys, surfaces), positions, velocities, and accelerations are related by constraints.

**Inextensible String Constraint:**
If a string is inextensible, the total length of the string remains constant.
Differentiating the length equation gives velocity and acceleration constraints.

**Common Cases:**
1. Two blocks connected by string over a pulley: Both have the same magnitude of acceleration
2. String passing over multiple pulleys: Use the constraint that total string length is constant
3. Wedge-block system: Relative acceleration along the contact surface

## 13. Circular Motion Dynamics

### Centripetal Force
For a body moving in a circle of radius r with speed v:
**F_c = mv²/r = mω²r** (directed toward the center)

This is NOT a new type of force. It is provided by existing forces (tension, gravity, friction, normal force, etc.)

### Examples of Centripetal Force
1. **Satellite/Planet**: Gravity provides centripetal force
2. **Car on a flat road**: Friction provides centripetal force
3. **Ball on a string (horizontal circle)**: Component of tension provides centripetal force
4. **Car on a banked road**: Components of normal force and friction provide centripetal force

### Banking of Roads
For a car on a banked road at angle θ:
- **Without friction**: tan θ = v²/(rg) → v = √(rg tanθ)
- **With friction (maximum speed)**: v_max = √[rg(μ + tanθ)/(1 - μtanθ)]
- **With friction (minimum speed)**: v_min = √[rg(tanθ - μ)/(1 + μtanθ)]

### Vertical Circular Motion (Non-uniform)
For a body on a string in a vertical circle of radius r:

**At the top:**
- T_top + mg = mv²_top/r
- Minimum speed at top for complete circle: v_top = √(gr) (when T = 0)

**At the bottom:**
- T_bottom - mg = mv²_bottom/r
- Minimum speed at bottom: v_bottom = √(5gr)

**At any angle θ from bottom:**
- T - mg cosθ = mv²/r
- Using energy conservation: v² = v²_bottom - 2gr(1 - cosθ)

**Tension difference:**
T_bottom - T_top = 6mg (independent of speed)

## 14. Important Problem-Solving Strategies for JEE

### Step-by-Step Approach
1. **Read the problem carefully**: Identify what's given and what's asked
2. **Draw a clear diagram**: Show all objects and their interactions
3. **Draw FBD for each body**: Include all forces
4. **Choose coordinate axes**: Usually along and perpendicular to motion
5. **Apply Newton's Laws**: ΣF = ma for each body
6. **Write constraint equations**: If bodies are connected
7. **Solve equations simultaneously**: Get unknowns
8. **Check dimensions and limiting cases**: Verify answer makes sense

### Common JEE Problem Types
1. **Block on inclined plane** (with/without friction)
2. **Connected blocks** (strings and pulleys)
3. **Bodies in an elevator** (apparent weight problems)
4. **Circular motion** (banking, vertical circles)
5. **System of bodies** (finding acceleration and tension)
6. **Friction problems** (determining motion type first)
7. **Pseudo force problems** (non-inertial frames)

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Drawing forces applied BY the body instead of ON the body in FBD
- Forgetting to consider all forces (especially friction and normal force)
- Assuming static friction = μₛN (it is ≤ μₛN)
- Not checking whether a block actually moves before applying kinetic friction
- Incorrect sign conventions
- Forgetting that tension is same throughout a massless string
- Not accounting for pseudo forces in non-inertial frames
